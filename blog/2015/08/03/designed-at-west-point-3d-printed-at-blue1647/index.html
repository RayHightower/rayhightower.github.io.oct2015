Designed at West Point. 3D Printed at BLUE1647.

August 03, 2015

Designed at West Point. 3D-printed at BLUE1647 in Chicago.

Experimenting with Parallella? Then you might need a way to protect it. The original acrylic cases are no longer available. Fortunately, 3D printing offers a solid alternative.

A highly-expandable 3D-printable case was designed by a team at the United States Military Academy at West Point: Professor Suzanne J. Matthews and Master Machinist William (“Frank”) Blackmon. Professor Matthews uses Parallella in her high-performance computing courses West Point.

Simple, Expandable Design

The West Point case only has three parts! You combine the three parts at will to accommodate multiple Parallella cluster configuration.

Mix and match the parts. Stack at will.

Internal Honeycomb Structure

Engineering details make a big difference in project like this. While the flat portions of the case are printing, the internal honeycomb structure is visible. I suspect that the honeycomb gives the panels greater strength while reducing the material cost and shortening the print time. Clever engineering at work.

Internal honeycomb structure.

3D Printing Process (w/gotchas)

You can grab the STereoLithography (STL) files from Thingiverse. I asked Patrick Harris of iMagine-it-Tech to guide me through the process. Here are a few things I learned from iMagine-it-Tech:

  • If the build plate has room for multiple copies of your object, print them. Here’s why: 3D-printing is in its infancy and results are less than perfect. If a part does not come out as designed, and you’ve printed multiple copies, you might have an extra to fall back on.

Print multiple copies when space allows.

  • Keep the build plate absolutely level. The build plate is the platform where MakerBot deposits material to form the 3D-printed object. If the build plate is even slightly skewed, you could end up with spaghetti-fication, strings of filament all over the place. Not fun.

  • Print with the flat-side of the object down against the build plate. 3D printing does not do well with overhangs.

  • Whether you own your own 3D printer or not, you might want to grab a copy of the MakerBot Desktop software. Versions are available for Mac OS X, Windows, and Linux. The software will let you view and manipulate STL files at will.

After Printing

The Dremel Tool has become one of the most valuable tools in my maker kit. For this project, it was helpful to use the Dremel for edge-smoothing and screw-shortening.

Trim rough edges with a Dremel and a razor blade.

Screws Required

You can secure the Parallella circuit board to the bottom side of the case with four sheet metal screws, size #4. The 3/8” screws extended beyond the bottom of the case, so I trimmed them a bit with a Dremel.

Sheet metal screws (#4). Left screw was shortened with a Dremel.

Conclusion

This is the best 3D-printed case for Parallella that I have found so far, mainly because it is stackable and expandable.

Even better: Professor Matthews is building a body of instructional materials that focus on parallel processing. I look forward to hearing more from this team.
